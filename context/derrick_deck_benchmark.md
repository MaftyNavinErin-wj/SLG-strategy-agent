# Derrick SLG Mountain Deck As Research Benchmark

Status: accepted internal benchmark

Deck date: 2025-09

Benchmark adopted: 2026-07-23

Source: `sources/reference_decks/derrick_slg_mountain_20250923.pdf`

## Role In This Workspace

The Derrick deck represents the September 2025 IEG baseline view of the SLG category. It combines a mountain taxonomy, product and team judgment, market estimates, activity curves, system comparisons, and capability implications.

It should be treated as:

1. The starting benchmark for SLG mountain work.
2. A high-quality prior built from deeper category work than a normal public-source summary.
3. A dated research artifact containing estimates and judgments, not an unquestionable or timeless truth.

New research should aim to exceed the baseline in one of two ways:

1. More fundamental: explain why the observed split exists, as the A x G x R constraint model does.
2. More granular: add mechanics, payer segmentation, ARPU / ARPPU, retention cohorts, season curves, alliance behavior, or live-ops equilibrium evidence.

## Baseline Conclusions To Preserve

The deck's most important structural conclusions are:

1. COK Like and Lutu Like are the two main SLG mountains.
2. COK Like uses a coordinate-oriented map, vertical growth, resource / acceleration sales, cross-server matching, and a head-and-body alliance hierarchy.
3. Lutu Like uses a path-oriented geo-board, season reset, horizontal general / skill combinations, alliance discipline, diverse organization roles, and cross-season social continuity.
4. ROK adds free march and real-time battlefield operation while retaining much of the COK-side long-account and vertical-growth structure.
5. Casual COK changes user entry and UA efficiency without necessarily weakening the monetization depth of the SLG backend.
6. Fusion products attempt to combine ROK battlefield expression with Lutu-side card and season logic, but their long-term social and economic stability remains difficult.
7. Product outcomes depend not only on core design but also on traffic access, UA infrastructure, content production, long-term operation, matching, and social-system capability.

## Alignment With The A x G x R Framework

The deck is strongly consistent with the current internal taxonomy.

| Deck conclusion | Deck pages | A x G x R interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| COK sells vertical growth and resources | 3, 23 | Low-to-medium geo constraint and weak reset permit durable vertical power; attrition and economic sinks constrain repeated use |
| Lutu sells horizontal card combinations | 3, 22, 28-29 | High geo constraint and strong reset reward contextual answers and renew roster demand |
| COK alliance uses head and body | 30 | High attrition creates ordinary-player body value around rally leads and high-power accounts |
| Lutu alliance has diverse roles and high strategic complexity | 30 | High geo dependency makes organization the network through which power reaches the map |
| Lutu preserves paid assets but resets grind and map results | 21-22 | Reset constrains temporal persistence at world-order and seasonal-economy layers while retaining paid ownership |
| ROK has real-time combat but remains closer to COK | 3, 23-25 | Real-time operation modifies expression; it does not replace the underlying attrition / long-account structure |
| Return to Empire is too grindy / expensive and has weak social sedimentation | 4, 29, 31 | High in-season consumption, strong seasonal depreciation, and weaker organization value can stack pressure without producing a stable social contract |

The A x G x R model is an extension because it explains the deck's observed differences through three permissions of accumulated power:

1. Reuse: how often power can be used before attrition and recovery matter.
2. Reach: where power can be projected before geography and organization matter.
3. Persistence: how long power and established success remain useful before reset matters.

Vertical versus horizontal power and monetization are downstream structural results, not additional primary axes.

## Important Refinements To Our Prior Output Hypotheses

The deck corrects several oversimplifications in the first output matrix:

1. Do not combine classic COK, casual COK, and ROK into one output profile. They share a vertical / long-account backend, but have materially different acquisition, activity, and revenue structures.
2. Do not assume casual COK trades monetization depth for broader reach. Page 11 shows its 2024 overseas revenue share exceeded its download and MAU shares, which the deck reads as payment depth not weaker than classic COK.
3. Lutu-like season cyclicality is visible in activity, but mature products can maintain a stable core. Pages 16-18 show strong season-start usage spikes for `三国谋定天下`, a more stable but declining `三国志战略版` base, and a smaller, stable `率土之滨` core.
4. Fusion risk is supported by both judgment and product outcomes. The deck records strong initial revenue for Return to Empire, followed by weaker long-term scale, and explicitly attributes social difficulty to real-time battle compressing pre-war planning and manager / diplomat value.
5. Low entry cost does not imply low monetization depth. Page 29 shows `三国谋定天下` lowering draw price and formation thresholds while extending red-star and skill depth to a high total graduation requirement.

Detailed output hypotheses and numeric anchors are in `context/slg_output_hypotheses.md`.

## Discrepancy Protocol

When new work disagrees with the deck, record:

1. The new claim.
2. The exact deck page and baseline claim.
3. Whether the disagreement is about definition, causal explanation, data, time period, geography, product version, or level of abstraction.
4. The evidence quality on each side.
5. Whether the new work confirms, refines, or supersedes the baseline.

Do not silently rewrite the baseline. A discrepancy is often a useful research question.

## Source Limits

1. Market and activity figures are estimates, not audited company disclosures.
2. 2025E values are forecasts made around September 2025.
3. Product and regional classifications reflect the deck's taxonomy and may need restatement under A x G x R.
4. Activity charts are valuable for curve shape but do not substitute for cohort retention, payer retention, or server-level causal analysis.
5. The deck has limited direct ARPU, ARPPU, payer-concentration, and retention-cohort data. Those remain research gaps.
