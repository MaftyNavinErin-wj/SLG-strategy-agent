# SLG Deck Digest

Source deck: `SLG山头讨论_20250923.pdf`

This is a distilled working note for future conversations. It is not a page-by-page transcript.

## One-Line Read

Mobile SLG is a multiplayer online war simulation built on 4X, big-map PVP / GVG, season or server cycles, and social organization. The key split is not theme, but the underlying design model: COK Like vs. 率土 Like, with ROK / fusion and casual COK Like as important branches.

## What SLG Really Is

SLG is not just strategy combat. It is:

1. Limited resources.
2. Infinite conflict.
3. Individual growth.
4. Alliance protection and discipline.
5. PVP validation.
6. GVG war and diplomacy.
7. Endgame pursuit: dominate the server or earn status inside an alliance.

The deck's important conceptual point:

> SLG is a war simulation and a reflection of jungle society. Realism / immersion does not come only from theme or art; it comes from systems that make resource, territory, alliance, war, hierarchy, and honor feel credible.

## Main Mountains

### COK Like

Core model:

1. Coordinate-based big map.
2. City building, resources, army growth.
3. Individual power and whale leadership matter.
4. Alliance structure is looser than 率土 Like.
5. Cross-server war and event calendar absorb numerical inflation.
6. Monetization leans vertical: heroes, skills, speedups, resources.

Player fantasy:

1. I am a lord / leader / whale / war driver.
2. I lead, attack, raid, and compete for server dominance.

Recent growth driver:

1. Casual COK Like.
2. Casual entry lowers UA cost.
3. Casual gameplay gradually integrates into the resource loop.
4. Late-game SLG systems convert broader users into higher-LTV players.

Representative direction: Whiteout Survival, Last War: Survival, Kingshot.

### 率土 Like

Core model:

1. Path / territory-based map.
2. Season system.
3. Geopolitical expansion and chokepoints.
4. Horizontal general / skill buildcraft.
5. Alliance discipline, diplomacy, division of labor.
6. Monetization leans card draw and horizontal combinations.

Player fantasy:

1. I build status and achievement inside an alliance.
2. Strategy, execution, diplomacy, routing, sieges, and season outcomes create identity.

Key challenge:

1. Player cognitive ceiling.
2. Season rules, maps, battle reports, and UX must reduce early friction while preserving deep strategic value.

Important read on 三国谋定天下:

1. It lowers friction in operation, season rhythm, and draw experience.
2. It does not necessarily lower total depth.
3. Complexity is shifted away from early operation into later buildcraft, season environment, and progression depth.

## Six-Layer SLG Teardown Framework

Use this for any SLG product.

### 1. Immersion / Substitution

Questions:

1. Who does the player become?
2. A lord, general, alliance officer, diplomat, conqueror, survivor, or state-builder?
3. What motivates conquest?
4. How does theme map to systems and monetization?

### 2. Server / Season Loop

Questions:

1. What resets?
2. What is preserved?
3. Does the season solve numerical inflation, player cognition, or social re-matching?
4. Is the loop driven by gameplay, event calendar, or cross-server matching?

Rule of thumb from the deck:

1. Paid honor / permanent value tends to be preserved.
2. Grind / map control / seasonal ranking tends to reset.
3. Resettable parts preserve future balancing room.

### 3. Map Design

Questions:

1. Coordinate map or path / territory map?
2. What are the point, line, and surface relationships?
3. Does the map only show information, or does it create strategic decisions?
4. Does LOD / zoom level create real strategy or just visual expression?

### 4. Combat Strategy

Questions:

1. Is combat mostly power-value comparison?
2. Is strategy in pre-battle composition, live command, terrain, timing, or battle report interpretation?
3. How does combat feed monetization?
4. How much does the player understand why they won or lost?

### 5. Social Structure

Questions:

1. Why do players ally?
2. Is leadership whale-driven, officer-driven, or system-assigned?
3. What roles exist: leader, officer, strategist, diplomat, scout, logistics, executor?
4. Can social relationships persist across seasons?
5. Does the system support or damage alliance operators?

### 6. User Acquisition / Traffic

Questions:

1. Is the product buying payers or converting broad casual traffic?
2. Which channel matters: Applovin, TikTok / Douyin, Bilibili, mini-game platforms, web, PC emulator?
3. Are ad creatives an honest expression of the product loop?
4. Does the team have UA infrastructure: campaign setup, in-flight optimization, postmortem, material asset management, attribution, and data engineering?

## Key Strategic Reads

### High-Power Constraint As The Deeper Split

The deck points to this through reset rules, map model, COK "head and body" social structure, Lutu-like division of labor, and fusion social-retention challenges, but it does not fully name the underlying mechanism.

Current workspace read:

1. The genre-defining question is how the system constrains high-power players and therefore gives ordinary players value.
2. COK / ROK-like systems mainly constrain high-power players through troop / body attrition, economic sinks, matching, and event cadence. Ordinary players mainly provide attrition / body value.
3. Lutu-like systems mainly constrain high-power players through geo dependency, season reset, route access, and organization. Ordinary players mainly provide logistics / organization value.
4. Real-time battlefield operation is a powerful expression and burden modifier, especially for ROK and fusion, but it is not the root taxonomy axis.

### Casual COK Like Growth

Casual COK Like growth is not just "SLG plus minigame." It is a user-entry and traffic-efficiency shift:

1. IDFA weakened classic payer-buying strategies.
2. Casual gameplay reduced acquisition cost.
3. Ad platforms and attribution systems improved traffic efficiency.
4. Casual entry brought broader users.
5. Late SLG alliance / war / progression systems captured LTV.

### Fusion SLG Risk

Fusion SLG often tries to combine ROK-style map / battle expression with 率土-style card buildcraft and monetization.

Main tension:

1. If too hard-core: it requires both strategic understanding and deep monetization tolerance.
2. If too casual: long-term economy, social structure, and strategic identity may not hold.
3. Real-time combat can compress pre-war planning and reduce the value of officers / diplomats, hurting social retention.

### Team Capability

Good SLG teams need:

1. System design: resources, season, map, combat, alliance, monetization must lock together.
2. MMO / social operation experience: alliance, migration, matching, merging, anti-cheat, black-market control.
3. Map and combat innovation: not new skin, but new strategic dimension.
4. UA / Marketing Tech: creative, buying, attribution, data engineering, product-publishing feedback loop.
5. Live ops: seasons, events, cross-server war, balance, player segmentation, non-GVG retention.
6. Deep understanding of theme and player substitution: theme is a system and user-entry problem, not just a skin.
